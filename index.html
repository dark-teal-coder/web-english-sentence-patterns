Basic and Advanced English Sentence Patterns

B1. When ...

Model

  1. When he opened the door, he saw his girlfriend.
  2. He saw his girlfriend when he opened the door.
  3. When he was taking a bath, his brother opened the door.
  4. When he had completed the report, he put it in an envelope.

Grammar

  1. When is a conjunction. It is used to link two clauses.
  2. 'When' can be put at the beginning of the first clause or the second clause.
  3. Special care should be paid to the correct tenses of the verbs used.
  4. An action which lasted a very short moment is in the past simple tense.
  5. An action which lasted a longer time is in the past continuous tense.
  6. When one action occurred before the other, the past perfect tense is used.

Usage

It is used to describe two actions happening at the same time, or one after another.

  10. The police searched for the criminal. During the search, the criminal opened fire.

B2. While ...

Model

  1. While she was waiting for the train, it was raining.
  2. He was sleeping while his sister was doing homework.

Grammar

  1. While is a conjunction. It is used to link two clauses.
  2. Usually, when both actions in the sentence continued for some time, we use the past continuous tense.

Usage

  1. It is used to describe two actions which happened at the same moment and continued for some time.
  2. "When" can be used. "While" is more formal.

B3. After + DOING ...

Model

  1. After hearing the bad news, she fainted.
  2. Before setting out, they checked their luggage.
  3. While watching the play, he found that his purse was lost.

Grammar

  1. After, before, while are prepositions in these patterns.
  2. They are followed by present participles.
  3. The subject of the participle in this pattern must be the same as the subject in the main clause.

Usage

  1. These patterns emphasize the time when something happened.
  2. It is common in written style.
  3. After seeing = Having seen

B4. not only ... but also ...

Model

  1. He not only promised to fulfill the conditions, but he (ahe signed an undertaking to that effect.
  2. He promised not only to give spiritual support but also finance our campaign.
  3. He saw not only the parents but also their sons.

Grammar

  1. Not only... but also should most be used in a pair.

  3. Sentence a is wrong because the phrases following not only and but also are not the same in grammatical construction Sentence b is correct because speak English well is paralle to write fluent English.

Usage

  1. This pattern is used to emphasize the importance of both ideas in the sentence.

B5. Although...

Model

  1. Although the test was difficult, he managed to score full marks.
  2. He managed to score full marks although the test was difficult.
  3. The test was difficult, but he managed to score full marks.

Grammar

  1. Although and but are both conjunctions.
  2. Although can be placed at the beginning or after the main clause.
  3. When but is placed at the beginning, it is an adverb, not a conjunction.
  4. Common mistakes:
    1. Although he was hard-working, but he failed. (Wrong)
    2. But he was hard-working, he failed. (Wrong)
    3. Although he was hard-working, he failed, (Correct)

Usage

  1. The use of although and but is to prepare the reader for something unexpected.
  2. They are used in written as well as spoken English.

B6. Since / Because / As ...

Model

  1. Since he is not in the office, I shall leave this message to him.
  2. Because the chairman was absent, the meeting was called off.
  3. As he disliked smoking, he chose a seat in the non-smoking zone.

Grammar

  1. They are conjunctions.
  2. They can be put at the beginning of a sentence or after the main clause.
  3. Common mistake:
    • e.g. He was absent. Because he was ill. (Wrong)

Usage

  1. They introduce reasons.
  2. They are used in written as well as spoken English.
  3. The pattern with since, because or as put at the beginning is slightly more formal than that with the words put after the main clause.

B7. The ...

Model

  1. To pursue higher qualifications, he studies very hard.
  2. To earn a living, he works from morning till night.

Grammar

  1. This pattern starts with an infinitive.
  2. To can be replaced by in order to.

Usage

  1. The to-phrase introduces the purpose of doing something.
  2. This pattern is used both in spoken and written English.
  3. In order to is more formal.

Grammar

  1. In order to can be placed at the beginning of a sentence or after the main clause while so as to can only be placed after the main clause.
  2. The two phrases must be followed by an infinitive.

Usage

  1. They have the meaning of 'with the purpose of.
  2. In order to is more formal.

  1. On condition that is a conjunction.

  3. This conjunction is rarely put at the beginning of a sentence.

Usage

  1. It lays down a condition to which other people must agree.
  2. Usually, the future aspect is used in the main clause: the simple present tense is used in the subordinate clause.

B12. It is + adjective + to ...

Model

  2. It is impossible to trust the word of a habitual liar.

Grammar

  1. The pattern starts with it is + adjective + to...
  2. An infinitive must be used after this expression.

Usage

  1. This expression is used to comment on an action.

    • I am stupid. I forgot your name.

B15. Gerund ...

Model

  1. Winning is important but breaking the Hong Kong record, is what really matters to me.
  2. Playing war games is popular among young people nowadayı

Grammar

  1. This pattern starts with a gerund.
  2. A gerund has the property of a noun. It is the subject of a sentence and can be preceded by adjectives.

  3. It has the property of a verb. It can take an object and is modified by adverbs.

Usage

  1. Using gerunds is a good way of varying style, particularly written style.

B20. who/which / that (Defining clause)

Model

  1. The umbrella which / that has a broken handle is mine.
  2. People who/ that eat too much die early.

Grammar

  1. Who and which are relative pronouns.
  2. Who is used for persons; which is used for things; that is used for both persons and things.
  3. The clause after which or who in this pattern is called a defining clause. It says which particular one or group.
  4. Defining clauses do not require commas to separate them from the rest of the sentence.
    • e.g. He tells a tale, that sounds untrue. (Wrong)
    • He tells a tale which / that sounds untrue. (Correct)

Usage

  1. This is a very common pattern in written English.
  2. That as a relative pronoun is informal; who and which are more formal.

A30. So ... that

Model

  2. So great a fire raged that the building was burnt down.

Grammar

  1. This pattern starts with so. that.
  2. An adverb or adjective can be put after so.
  3. If an adverb follows so, the special verb should be placed before the subject.

Usage

  1. So... that introduces a result.
  2. This pattern is used to emphasize the cause.
  3. It is formal, and is rarely used in spoken English.

A32. No sooner... than /Hardly... when

Model

  1. No sooner had the police arrived than the crowd dispersed.
  2. Hardly had the police arrived when the crowd dispersed.

Grammar

  1. These are inverted patterns.
  2. "No sooner... than" and "hardly (scarcely)... when" use the same construction.
  3. The past perfect tense is used after no sooner or hardly; the past simple tense is used after than or when.
  4. Only the past perfect tense can be used after hardly.

Usage

  1. These patterns can be used to suggest that one thing hap pened very soon after another. Therefore, the past perfect tense is often used.
  2. No sooner means as soon as or immediately; hardly means just after.
  3. These patterns are used in a formal and literary style.

A33. Parallel structures

Model

  1. Those who wish to write well must learn to think logically. to organize ideas coherently, and to express such ideas clearly.
  2. Logical thought, coherent organization, and clear express-ion are the main ingredients of effective writing.

Grammar

  1. These are what we call "parallel structures".
  2. Similar ideas are put into similar grammatical constructions. If one idea in a sentence is expressed by a phrase, other equivalent ideas in a sentence are expressed by phrases. If one idea is expressed by an infinitive or a gerund or a clause, other equivalent ideas should be presented in similar constructions.

Usage

  1. These patterns are formal, often used in speeches.
  2. The aim of using parallel structures is to achieve neatness in style.
