Basic and Advanced English Sentence Patterns

B1. When ...

Model

  1. When he opened the door, he saw his girlfriend.
  2. He saw his girlfriend when he opened the door.
  3. When he was taking a bath, his brother opened the door.
  4. When he had completed the report, he put it in an envelope.

Grammar

  1. When is a conjunction. It is used to link two clauses.
  2. 'When' can be put at the beginning of the first clause or the second clause.
  3. Special care should be paid to the correct tenses of the verbs used.
  4. An action which lasted a very short moment is in the past simple tense.
  5. An action which lasted a longer time is in the past continuous tense.
  6. When one action occurred before the other, the past perfect tense is used.

Usage

It is used to describe two actions happening at the same time, or one after another.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences. Start with when.

  1. He left. He did not say goodbye.
  2. I went on a picnic yesterday. I saw many butterflies on a tree.
  3. He waited for his friend. He saw a robbery.
  4. He photo-copied the answers first. Then he returned the book to the library.
  5. He had a nightmare. At that moment, the bell rang.
  6. Peter drove to Shatin last Sunday. Halfway there the car broke down.
  7. Peter went to a concert last night. During the performance, the lights broke went out.
  8. He made sure that the answer was correct. Then he copied it in his book.
  9. Mary had an English lesson yesterday. During the lesson, her mother came to the school.
  10. The police searched for the criminal. During the search, the criminal opened fire.

B2. While ...

Model

  1. While she was waiting for the train, it was raining.
  2. He was sleeping while his sister was doing homework.

Grammar

  1. While is a conjunction. It is used to link two clauses.
  2. Usually, when both actions in the sentence continued for some time, we use the past continuous tense.

Usage

  1. It is used to describe two actions which happened at the same moment and continued for some time.
  2. "When" can be used. "While" is more formal.

B3. After + DOING ...

Model

  1. After hearing the bad news, she fainted.
  2. Before setting out, they checked their luggage.
  3. While watching the play, he found that his purse was lost.

Grammar

  1. After, before, while are prepositions in these patterns.
  2. They are followed by present participles.
  3. The subject of the participle in this pattern must be the same as the subject in the main clause.

Usage

  1. These patterns emphasize the time when something happened.
  2. It is common in written style.
  3. After seeing = Having seen

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. After we had taken off our shoes, we waded through the water.
  2. When he knew that he was late, he was very disappointed.
  3. While he was playing football, he remembered he had an important appointment.
  4. When he felt tired, he usually took a hot bath.
  5. Just as he had locked the door, he found that he had forgotten to bring his key.
  6. After he had failed in the examination, he felt terribly ashamed.
  7. After she had changed into her swimming suit, she jumped into the blue cool sea.
  8. When she saw the danger, she warned the people to leave.
  9. After he had received the news of his friend's death, he burst into tears.
  10. When she was eating silently in the kitchen, her master discovered her.

B4. not only ... but also ...

Model

  1. He not only promised to fulfill the conditions, but he (ahe signed an undertaking to that effect.
  2. He promised not only to give spiritual support but also finance our campaign.
  3. He saw not only the parents but also their sons.
  4. He can not only speak good English but also write fluen English.

Grammar

  1. Not only... but also should most be used in a pair.
  2. The sentence is a parallel pattern: the two correlative should be followed by the same part of speech.
    1. He not only can speak English well but also writs fluent English. (wrong)
    2. He can not only speak English well but also write fluen English. (Correct)
  3. Sentence a is wrong because the phrases following not only and but also are not the same in grammatical construction Sentence b is correct because speak English well is paralle to write fluent English.

Usage

  1. This pattern is used to emphasize the importance of both ideas in the sentence.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using not only... but also:

  1. I could find many cassette tapes on the shelf. I could find many videos on the shelf.
  2. They ate a five-course meal; they also drank six bottles of wine.
  3. She is both a professional artist and a first-rate teacher.
  4. She likes watching football. She enjoys playing football.
  5. There was his blood on the knife. There were his fingerprints on the door.
  6. He has a first-class brain. He is also a tremendously hard worker.
  7. He regarded buying a house as a top priority and he was willing to spend all on the decorations of his house.
  8. Some people think camping more exciting. They actually prefer it to picnicking.
  9. She copied homework from her classmate. She told a lie to her teacher.
  10. He hates people wasting time. He dislikes people having idle talks.

B32. ...as if/as though...

Model

  1. She screamed hysterically as if/as though she had seen a ghost.
  2. He behaved as if/as though he were the owner of the house.
  3. She looks as if/as though she is going to cry.

Grammar

  1. As if and as though are conjunctions.
  2. They can only be put after the main clause.
  3. The clause after as if/as though can be in the present tense or the past tense.

Usage

  1. The two expressions are used in the same way.
  2. After as if and as though, we often use the past tense with a present meaning to show that comparison is 'unreal'.
    • e.g. They are talking as if they were old friends. (But they don't know each other.)

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. They looked at me. It made me feel that I was mad.
  2. She behaved in a very strange way. I wondered if she had stolen something.
  3. The sales girl talked to me very rudely. It seemed that she was the customer.
  4. The little child is walking. I am afraid she is going to fall at any time.
  5. Why is she looking at me? It seems that she knows me but I am sure she doesn't.
  6. He swims very quickly like a fish.
  7. He speaks in a very bold manner. You may think he knows everything in the world, but I am sure he doesn't.
  8. It is cloudy. I think it is going to rain.
  9. She spoke to everyone coldly. She looked like an undertaker.
  10. He behaved proudly. He thought that he was a prince.

A1. Participle phrase

Model

  1. Candidates writing on alternate lines will lose marks.
  2. Young people in Hong Kong are fond of anything imported from Japan.
  3. Poor little Mary, looking frustrated and bewildered, stood in front of her father.
  4. The fifth annual meeting, chaired by the new director, was held in a hotel.

Grammar

  1. The phrases are called participle phrases.
  2. A participle phrase can start with a present or past participle.
  3. They are used after the nouns they qualify.
  4. The sentence may be defining (without commas), or non-defining (with commas).

Usage

  1. See defining and non-defining relative clauses.
  2. It is more concise than a pattern with relative clauses.
  3. When a present participle is used, the phrase is in the active voice; when a past participle is used, the phrase is in the passive voice.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. The van ran over a man. (The man was begging in the street.)
  2. The restaurant waiter was finally sacked. (He was impolite to many customers.)
  3. Most of the goods are of high quality. (These goods are sold in this department store.)
  4. The Clean Hong Kong Campaign does not take its desirable effect. (This campaign is launched nearly every year.)
  5. The two-hundred-year-old tower will be demolished next September. (It stands near the church.)
  6. Three masked robbers rushed into a bank in Central yesterday evening. (They were armed with machine guns.)
  7. All students must stay after school. (They broke the school rule.)
  8. The doctor gave her some tablets. (These tablets were taken four times a day.)
  9. The Rev. Yau's Scholarship is open to all candidates studying in Hong Kong. (The scholarship was founded by the son of the late pastor.)
  10. Drivers will be fined. (These drivers exceed the speed of seventy kilo-metres per hour.)

A2. Preposition + which / whom

Model

  1. The book for which I am looking is a computer menu.
  2. Doctor Lee, in whom they have complete confidence, has emigrated to Canada recently.

Grammar

  1. The preposition is placed before which or whom.
  2. The use of prepositions depends on the verb or noun in the relative clause.
    • e.g. The book is a computer menu. I am looking for the book.
      = The book for which I am looking is a computer menu.
  3. The relative clause can be a defining or a non-defining clause.
    • e.g. The boy with whom Mary did shopping yesterday is my cousin. (Defining)
    • e.g. Peter, with whom Mary did shopping yesterday, is my cousin. (Non-defining)

Usage

  1. It is used in formal writing.
    • e.g. The doctor in whom they have complete confidence has emigrated to Canada. (Formal)
    • e.g. The doctor that (who) they have complete confidence in has emigrated to Canada. (Informal)

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. Peter works in our office. I was at school with Peter.
  2. The owner of the restaurant gave us our money back. We complained to the owner.
  3. The magazines are surprisingly cheap. We read the latest reports in them.
  4. The captain was a very experienced pilot. They dined at the captain's table.
  5. Public opinion is often important. The Government pays no attention to it.
  6. The widow died in an accident. Her children relied on her.
  7. The mystery would never be solved. We were all puzzled by it.
  8. The little child won the contest again. Mr and Mrs Chan are proud of him.
  9. The building was surrounded by the police. Three robbers had dashed into that building.
  10. The computer software was stolen last night. I worked out the answer with it.

A3. Adjective + preposition + noun

Model

  1. His voice, pregnant with emotion, seemed to hypnotize her.
  2. South China, successful in getting the champion, won applause from the football fans.

Grammar

  1. The phrase used after the noun is called an adjective phrase. It is used to qualify the noun before it.
  2. The construction of an adjective phrase in this pattern is adjective + preposition + noun.
  3. The clause can be defining or non-defining.

Usage

  1. This pattern is often used in written English.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using patterns above.

  1. The principal was unwilling to talk with the students. (He was occupied with his personal affairs.)
  2. The fat man formed a laughable scene. (He was clumsy at working with his needle.)
  3. The suspect would be on trial in June. (He was accused of murder.)
  4. The prisoner killed himself in the cell. (He was unhappy with his mis- fortune.)
  5. The suspect smiled in front of the camera. (He was acquitted of the murder charge.)
  6. Anybody should write to the company, if they are eligible for the scho- larship.
  7. The youngest candidate got the job easily. (He was proficient in English and Japanese.)
  8. The audience were all silent when the concert finished. (They were impressed with the superb performance of the pianist.)
  9. The government was criticized widely. (It was ignorant of the need of its people.)
  10. Susan dressed herself fastidiously. (She was conscious of her friend's opinion about her.)

A4. ...so + adjective phrase + that ...

Model

  1. He was so fed up with his work that he desperately needed a holiday.
  2. His parents were so disappointed with his results that they asked him to quit school.

Grammar

  1. The expression so... that is a conjunction.
  2. An adjective phrase, instead of an adjective is put betwee so and that in this pattern.

Usage

  1. This pattern is more advanced and formal in writing.
  2. The so... that expression introduces a result.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. He was mad with rage. He killed his friend.
  2. His voice was pregnant with emotion. She was greatly impressed.
  3. The fat man was very clumsy with his needle. It formed a laughable scene.
  4. The candidate beat his opponents easily because he was very proficient in English and French.
  5. The audience were very impressed with the superb performance of the pianist. They began to shed tears.
  6. The government was criticized widely because it was ignorant of the need of its people.
  7. Mary was extremely conscious of her friend's opinion about her. So she dressed herself fastidiously.
  8. I was amazed by what I had heard. I stood there speechlessly.
  9. He was very competent for the task. Therefore he was elected chairman of the committee.
  10. The sales assistant was very discourteous to his customers. He was finally sacked.

A5. ...if not ...

Model

  1. It is ridiculous, if not absurd, to see the attitude of the old people towards sex.
  2. Smoking in public places is certainly anti-social, if not criminal.
  3. It is a pity, if not a disgrace that he failed the examination two times.

Grammar

  1. The word following if not should be of the same part of speech as the word it compares with.
    • e.g. It is ridiculous, if not an absurd thing, to see... (Bad in style)
    • e.g. It is ridiculous, if not absurd, to see.... (Good)
  2. If not should immediately follow the expression it wants to compare with.

Usage

  1. This is a more advanced pattern than the pattern starting with "it is adjective to / that..."
  2. These patterns are used in written style.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. It is disappointing to see that the Government cannot discourage young people from smoking.
  2. It is surprising to receive the news of his death.
  3. It is not likely to see any improvement to the problem.
  4. The insurance company's increase of premiums on cars is absolutely unfair.
  5. He is a bit passive in his studies.
  6. It is unusual to see gentlemen in top hats nowadays.
  7. It was unexpected that John told us of his divorce.
  8. Seeing a pair of horses in London streets is uncommon.
  9. It is funny to see the faces some men make when they are shaving.
  10. It was odd that you never noticed the difference between the boys.

Hints:

  • despairing
  • shocking
  • incredible
  • ridiculous
  • unreasonable
  • apathetic
  • impossible
  • rare

A6. When, etc. + past participle ...

Model

  1. When asked about his last job, he said he had been a mechanic.
  2. The medicine would not be effective if kept for long.
  3. Though warned of the danger, she still went skating.

Grammar

  1. In this pattern, the past participles go with suitable connectives to express the following meanings:
    1. time when, whenever, while, once and until
    2. place where, wherever
    3. condition if, unless
    4. concession though, although, even though

Usage

  1. This pattern is often used in written English.
  2. It is still a simple sentence, though skilfully constructed.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. If you do not speak to him, the child never speaks. (until)
  2. Unless the enemies attacked us, we would not take any action. (unless)
  3. They did not give in although they were defeated for the second time. (even though)
  4. When we found mosquitoes in a place, they should be exterminated. (where)
  5. I was warned by my colleagues it might be worse in the evening. I was therefore careful to leave for home early. (once)
  6. They were beaten by the triads. They reported the case to the police. (after)
  7. He was defeated many times, but he did not give up. (even though)
  8. He was impressed with their situation. He did not intend to help them. (though)
  9. Usually when he was attracted by something, he would open his eyes wide. (whenever)
  10. If he was not warned a second time, he would still smoke in the office. (unless)

A7. That...

Model

  1. That men and women did not enjoy equal rights in the past is quite obvious.
  2. That the Government wants to increase its revenue is clear for all to see.

Grammar

  1. That is a conjunction placed at the beginning of a sentence.
  2. The clause following that must be a complete statement.
  3. In model (a) that refers to the statement (men and women did not enjoy equal rights in the past). The remark is '(This fact) is quite obvious.'

Usage

  1. It is used in formal writing.
  2. It is used to emphasize the idea mentioned (in model (b) 'the Government wants to increase its revenue').
  3. It is common to make this mistake:
    1. The Government wants to increase its revenue is clear for all to see. (Wrong)
    2. That the Government wants to increase its revenue is clear for all to see. (Correct)

Practice

Combine the following sentences, beginning with that.

  1. People were more industrious in the past. It is doubtless.
  2. Students participate in more extra-curricular activities nowadays. It is encouraging.
  3. Students study only for examinations. It is extremely disappointing.
  4. Not all people have the opportunity to receive education. It is sad.
  5. The standard of living has risen substantially. It is undeniable.
  6. The price of petrol will continue to rise. It is certain.
  7. Hong Kong will be more prosperous and stable after ten years. It is virtually beyond question.
  8. Reading novels can improve our English. It is generally accepted.
  9. Television does more harm than good. It is evident.
  10. The standards of English will continue to fall. It is unavoidable.

A8. Whether ...

Model

  1. Whether (or not) a new incinerator should be constructed Hong Kong has been a controversial topic of discussion recently.
  2. Whether (or not) mercy killing should be legalized is widely argued.

Grammar

  1. Whether is a conjunction placed at the beginning of a sentence.
  2. The clause following whether must be a complete clause.
  3. In model (a) whether refers to the question 'Should a new airport be constructed in Hong Kong?" The remark is '(This question) is widely argued.'

Usage

  1. It is used in formal writing.
  2. 'or nor' can be omitted.
  3. It is common to make these mistakes:
    1. Whether a new incinerator should be constructed in Hong Kong. (Wrong)
    2. Should a new incinerator be constructed in Hong Kong is widely argued. (Wrong)
    3. Whether a new incinerator should be constructed in Hong Kong is widely argued. (Correct)

Practice

Combine the following sentences, beginning with whether.

  1. Has nuclear energy been a good or bad invention? It seems to be entirely a matter of opinion.
  2. Did students use to be more hard-working in the past? It is controversial.
  3. Will Hong Kong maintain stability and prosperity? It seems questionable.
  4. Will the tax on tobacco be doubled next year? It is hard to estimate.
  5. Is there life after death? The answer is still a mystery.
  6. Are there ghosts in this world? It is unknown.
  7. Should the teachers be blamed for the students' poor standard of English? It is debated endlessly.
  8. Is she the most beautiful girl in the beauty pageant? It is difficult to judge.
  9. Should he extend his help to them? It is at his own discretion.
  10. Will the library be open at weekends? It is being discussed.

A9. In spite of / Despite ...

Model

  1. In spite of/ Despite his numerous attempts, he failed.
  2. He is an efficient worker in spite of / despite his indiscipline / (his) lack of discipline / (his) being so undisciplined.
  3. In spite of/ Despite what he said, I would not believe him.

Grammar

  1. In spite of or despite can be put at the beginning or the middle of the sentence.
  2. They are prepositions.
  3. They must be followed by a noun, a noun phrase, or a noun clause.

Usage

  1. It is used more often in written English.
  2. Avoid using 'in spite of/ despite the fact that... too often because the phrase can simply be replaced by although.
  3. It is common to make these mistakes:
    1. Despite of his numerous attempts, he failed. (Wrong)
    2. In spite of he was hard-working, he failed. (Wrong)

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using in spite of /despite.

  1. Although he is trying to help her, she continues to grumble about the life she leads. (use 'attempt')
  2. Although the weather was hot, our holiday turned out to be satisfactory.
  3. Although it was in excellent condition, the car was quite old. (use 'being')
  4. Although she knew he had ill intentions, she accepted his help.
  5. Although the Government has spent a staggering sum of money on anti- smoking campaigns, still little improvement can be seen.
  6. Although the doctor gave him advice, he kept on drinking.
  7. Although he looks pleasant, no one likes him.
  8. Although he was wearing expensive clothes, he could not win respect from the others.
  9. Although Harry was very well qualified for the job, he did not get it.
  10. Although the Government has made a lot of effort to promote road safety, many people die in traffic accidents every year.

A10. Notwithstanding ...

Model

  1. Notwithstanding his illness, he kept on smoking heavily.
  2. He kept on smoking heavily, notwithstanding his illness.
  3. He kept on smoking heavily, his illness notwithstanding.

Grammar

  1. Notwithstanding is a preposition.
  2. It is followed by a noun, a noun phrase, or a noun clause.
  3. It can be placed at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end of a sentence.

Usage

  1. It is the same as in spite of and despite, but it can be placed at the end of a sentence.
  2. It is more formal than in spite of and despite.

Practice

Rewrite the following sentences, using the patterns above.

  1. Although there is criticism from the public, the law will go into effect.
  2. He tackled the problem boldly although there was difficulty.
  3. He kept on smoking regardless of the doctor's advice.
  4. Although he has promised to help us, we do not believe him.
  5. Although the weather is bad, we will set out according to our plan.
  6. We did not care what she said. We were not discouraged.
  7. The traffic was busy, but we were able to arrive on time.
  8. It was noisy outside, but he was able to concentrate his attention on the examination.
  9. His intention was evil. Still she accepted his offer.
  10. Although his position was high, he spoke to everyone politely.

A11. Provided that ...

Model

  1. Provided (that) he hands in his work on time, he will not be punished.
  2. The accident rate will decrease provided (that) the people take proper precautions.

Grammar

  1. Provided that is a conjunction.
  2. It can be put at the beginning of a sentence or after the main clause.
  3. The main clause is usually in the future aspect whereas the subordinate clause (with provided that) is in the present tense.

Usage

  1. Provided that means 'if and only if.
  2. It is usually used to state a fact rather than lay down a condition.

A12. Now that ...

Model

  1. Now (that) the number of passengers is increasing, the MTR should reduce its fare.
  2. Now (that) almost every family has a DVD player, fewer people go to the cinema.

Grammar

  1. Now that is a conjunction.
  2. It is placed at the beginning of a sentence.
  3. The present tense is used in this pattern.

Usage

  1. It is used to express cause. It means 'since' or 'considering that'.
  2. 'that' can be omitted.
    • e.g. Now I've read Shakespeare's tragedies, I understand why you like Shakespeare.

Practice

  2. She can solve the problem herself. Therefore, she does not want to seek help from anybody.
  3. We can plan our summer holiday because the examination is over.

  5. Sea food does not agree with him. Thus, he eats vegetables very often.

  8. As the road is congested, we had better go home on foot.
  9. Because the book has been sold out, I can only photocopy it.
  10. I need not visit video game centres because I have bought an XBox.
